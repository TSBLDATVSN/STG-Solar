Kinetic Theory Explanation of Boyle's Law Boyleâ€™s Law states that for a fixed amount of gas at a constant temperature, the pressure of the gas is inversely proportional to its volume. This means that if the volume of the gas decreases, the pressure increases, and if the volume increases, the pressure decreases, as long as the temperature stays constant. How the Kinetic Theory Explains Boyle's Law: The Kinetic Theory of Gases helps explain why this happens by focusing on the behavior of gas particles (molecules or atoms). Gas Particles are in Constant Motion: Gas particles are always moving randomly in all directions. They collide with each other and the walls of the container. Each collision with the wall of the container creates a pressure. The more collisions there are, the higher the pressure. Effect of Volume on Collisions: When the volume of the gas decreases (meaning the space is getting smaller), the gas particles have less space to move around in. Because the gas molecules are still moving at the same speed (since the temperature is constant), they will collide with the walls more often. The more often the particles collide with the walls, the greater the pressure. This is because the pressure is created by the number of collisions per unit area of the container. Effect of Volume on Pressure: When the volume of the gas increases, the particles have more room to move around. As a result, the particles hit the walls less often. Fewer collisions mean the pressure decreases because the number of impacts on the walls decreases. Why the Relationship is Inverse: According to the Kinetic Theory, the pressure of the gas depends on how often and how forcefully the gas molecules hit the container walls. If the volume is reduced, the particles are forced into a smaller space, so they collide more frequently. This increases the pressure. If the volume increases, the particles have more space to spread out, causing fewer collisions and a decrease in pressure. Hence, pressure and volume are inversely proportional. If volume goes up, pressure goes down, and if volume goes down, pressure goes up.
